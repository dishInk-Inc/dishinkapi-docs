Purchase Tracking is the manager and owner's view of what you're buying, how often, and at what price. It's derived entirely from the **Adjustments log** — every restock is a purchase — so the two stay in perfect sync.

<Frame>
  ![Purchase tracking view grouped by supplier with spend and quantity summaries](/images/inventory/purchase-tracking.png)
</Frame>

## Who this is for

- **Owner** — monthly spend review, supplier comparison.
- **Manager** — cross-checks against supplier statements before payment.
- **Accountant** — cost-of-goods reporting.

## Three ways to read the data

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="By ingredient" icon="carrot">
    Total quantity received and total spend per ingredient over the selected period. Best for spotting price hikes.
  </Card>

  <Card title="By reference number" icon="file-invoice">
    One row per supplier invoice. Best for reconciliation against supplier statements.
  </Card>

  <Card title="By day / week / month" icon="calendar">
    Purchase spend over time. Best for cash-flow planning and identifying spend spikes.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Reviewing month-end

<Steps>
  <Step title="Filter to Restock, last month">
    On the Adjustments log, set type = **Restock** and date range = previous month.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Group by reference number">
    Each supplier invoice becomes a single row with total quantity and total cost across ingredients.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Export CSV">
    Hand to the accountant or import into your bookkeeping system.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Cross-check against supplier statements">
    Any invoice you were charged for that isn't in the export is a delivery someone didn't log.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Price-trend analysis

For any ingredient, tap **View history** on the ingredient row (Stock tab). You get a chart of unit cost over time — every restock plots a point. This is how you spot:

- Gradual supplier price creep
- One-off spikes from an emergency supplier
- Volume-discount thresholds

<Note>
  DishInk stores the unit cost at the moment of each restock. Changing the ingredient's saved unit cost later does not rewrite history — old restocks keep the cost you paid on the day.
</Note>

## Supplier comparison

If you buy the same ingredient from multiple suppliers, use different reference-number prefixes (e.g. `SPA-1234` for Supplier A, `SPB-9821` for Supplier B). Group the log by reference prefix in your export to compare average price per unit.

## Tips & best practices

<Tip>
  **Standardise reference numbers.** Agree with each supplier on a format and stick to it. Everything else in purchase tracking depends on those numbers.
</Tip>

<Tip>
  Review the top 5 ingredients by spend every month. A 5% price drop on your highest-volume ingredient is worth more than a 20% drop on a rarely used one.
</Tip>

## Common mistakes

- **Empty reference numbers** — a restock without a reference vanishes into a "no invoice" bucket at month-end.
- **Using Manual adjustment instead of Restock** — captures neither unit cost nor reference. Deliveries must be restocked, never adjusted.
- **Not tagging supplier** — without a reference prefix, you can't compare suppliers over time.

## Tips & best practices

<Tip>
  Enter POs the day of delivery, not weekly. Stock accuracy plummets when POs lag behind physical arrivals.
</Tip>

## Common mistakes

<Warning>
  Do not mark POs as Received before physically counting the delivery. Missing items become invisible variances.
</Warning>
